Chapter 3: The Structure of Argument

Understanding how arguments are structured is crucial for analyzing texts critically. This section introduces the main components: claims, evidence, and reasoning.

Study Note: Highlight claims and evidence in different colors when reading through these modules.

1. Claims

A claim is a statement the author asserts as true. It forms the backbone of the argument. A well-constructed claim must rely on logic rather than pure emotion.

  • Claims should always be specific and open to academic debate.
  • They must be supported directly by factual empirical evidence.

Visualizing the relationship between these concepts makes retention significantly simpler. The core claim is a target that requires concrete foundations to stand firmly on its own.

2. Evidence

Evidence provides support for a claim. Types include statistics, real-world examples, expert testimony, and empirical data collected from controlled scientific studies.

Self-Study Prompt: Identify one major claim and its supporting evidence from the last news article you read today.

3. Reasoning

Reasoning explains how evidence supports the claim. It often includes clear logical connections, direct comparisons, or cause-and-effect relationships.

4. Example of Reasoning

If changing weather patterns cause more frequent severe storms, then coastal infrastructure across major metropolitan areas must be systematically reinforced over the coming decade.

Quick Tip: Try mapping evidence directly to claims in a visual matrix or table to map out the author's train of thought.

5. Summary

When studying complex arguments across academic papers, focus on three primary tasks. First, isolate the main claims. Second, categorize the supporting evidence. Third, trace the underlying reasoning. Using diagrams and structured notes will reinforce your overall reading comprehension.
