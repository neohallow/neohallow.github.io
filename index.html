Chapter 3: The Structure of Argument

Introduction

Understanding how arguments are structured is crucial for analyzing texts critically. This section introduces the main components: claims, evidence, and reasoning.

Note: Highlight claims and evidence in different colors when studying.

1. Claims

A claim is a statement the author asserts as true. It forms the backbone of the argument.

  • Claims should be specific and debatable.
  • They are supported by evidence.
[Diagram: Claim → Evidence → Reasoning Flow]

2. Evidence

Evidence provides support for a claim. Types include statistics, examples, expert testimony, and empirical data.

Question: Identify one claim and its supporting evidence from the last article you read.

3. Reasoning

Reasoning explains how evidence supports the claim. It often includes logical connections, comparisons, or cause-effect relationships.

Example of Reasoning

If climate change causes more frequent storms (evidence), then coastal infrastructure must be reinforced (claim supported by reasoning).

Tip: Try mapping evidence to claims in a small table to visualize reasoning.

Summary

When studying arguments: 1) identify claims, 2) categorize supporting evidence, 3) trace reasoning. Use diagrams and notes to reinforce understanding.
