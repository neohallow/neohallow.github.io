Chapter 3: The Structure of Argument

Introduction

Understanding how arguments are structured is crucial for analyzing texts critically. This section introduces claims, evidence, and reasoning.

Note: Highlight claims and evidence in different colors when studying.

1. Claims

A claim is a statement the author asserts as true and it forms the backbone of an argument.

Claims should be specific, debatable, and supported by evidence.

2. Evidence

Evidence supports a claim using statistics, examples, expert testimony, or data.

Question: Identify one claim and its supporting evidence from the last article you read.

3. Reasoning

Reasoning explains how evidence supports a claim through logic, comparison, or cause and effect.

Example

If climate change increases storms, then coastal infrastructure must be reinforced.

Tip: Map evidence directly to claims in a simple list or table.

Summary

First identify claims, then match evidence, then trace reasoning step by step.
